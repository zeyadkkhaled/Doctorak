Acute Abdominal Pain: Clinical Perspectives and Urgency Assessment

Last updated: October 2023 Reviewed by: Dr. Sarah Johnson, MD

Clinical Summary

Acute abdominal pain represents 5-10% of emergency department visits. This article differentiates between benign etiologies and life-threatening conditions requiring immediate intervention, providing evidence-based triage guidelines for healthcare consumers.

While epigastric discomfort commonly results from benign conditions like dyspepsia, acute onset severe abdominal pain may indicate surgical emergencies including appendicitis (occurring in 7% of population) or bowel perforation with mortality rates exceeding 50% if untreated beyond 24 hours.

Time-Sensitive Conditions

Appendicitis

  • Prevalence: 8.6% males, 6.7% females lifetime risk
  • Classic Presentation: Periumbilical pain migrating to RLQ (McBurney's point), rebound tenderness
  • Emergency Indicators: Fever >38°C, leukocytosis >10,000/μL

Management: Immediate surgical consultation, NPO status, IV antibiotics

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Features Urgency Level
Acute Cholecystitis RUQ pain, Murphy's sign, fever Emergency

Red Flag Symptoms

  • Guarding/rigidity on palpation
  • Hemodynamic instability (SBP <90)
  • Hematemesis or melena
Immediate Action Required: Call emergency services if experiencing pain with syncope, hematochezia, or distension

Critical Body Temperature Thresholds: Clinical Implications

Updated: November 2023 Reviewed by: Dr. Michael Chen, MD

Clinical Overview

Body temperature abnormalities beyond 35°C (hypothermia) or above 41.5°C (hyperpyrexia) constitute medical emergencies. This article examines etiology, clinical presentation, and emergency interventions for critical thermoregulatory failures.

Physiological Norms

  • Normal Range: 36.1°C - 37.2°C (97°F - 99°F)
  • Clinical Fever: ≥38.0°C (100.4°F) rectal in children
  • Hyperpyrexia: >41.1°C (106°F) - Medical emergency
  • Hypothermia: <35°C (95°F) - Requires immediate intervention

Hyperpyrexia (>41.1°C/106°F)

Etiology

Infectious Causes

  • COVID-19/SARS-CoV-2
  • Bacterial sepsis (mortality 30-40%)
  • CNS infections (meningitis/encephalitis)

Non-Infectious Causes

  • Intracranial hemorrhage
  • Thyroid storm (mortality 20-30%)
  • Serotonin syndrome
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome
  • Heat stroke (mortality >50% if untreated)

Progressive Symptoms

Stage Clinical Manifestations
Early Profuse sweating, muscle cramps, nausea, extreme thirst
Intermediate Confusion, miosis, pallor, oliguria, vomiting
Late Coma, seizures, fixed dilated pupils, dry hot skin, respiratory failure

Pediatric Considerations

Normal Variations

  • Rectal/Ear/Forehead: ≥38.0°C (100.4°F)
  • Oral: ≥37.8°C (100°F)
  • Axillary: ≥37.2°C (99°F)

Emergency Indicators

  • Fever >40°C (104°F) in infants <3 months
  • Febrile seizures
  • Bulging fontanelle or neck stiffness
  • Petechial rash (possible meningococcemia)

Hypothermia (<35°C/95°F)

Etiology

  • Prolonged cold exposure
  • Cold water immersion
  • Endocrinopathies (hypothyroidism)
  • Neurodegenerative disorders
  • Psychotropic medications
  • Substance abuse (alcohol/opioids)

Clinical Presentation

  • Mild (32-35°C): Shivering, cold extremities
  • Moderate (28-32°C): AMS, bradycardia
  • Severe (<28°C): Coma, areflexia, apnea

Emergency Management

  1. Passive rewarming (remove wet clothing)
  2. Active external rewarming (heated blankets)
  3. Warm IV fluids (40-42°C)
  4. Cardiopulmonary monitoring
  5. ECG for Osborne waves (pathognomonic)
Contraindications: Alcohol/caffeine, vigorous massage, direct heat application

Evidence-Based References

  1. American College of Critical Care Medicine (2023). Thermoregulation Guidelines.
  2. Pediatrics Journal (2022). Febrile Illness Management in Children.
  3. Wilderness Medicine (2021). Accidental Hypothermia Protocols.

Chest Pain Differential Diagnosis: 5 Critical Etiologies

Updated: December 2023 Reviewed by: Dr. Emily Wilson, MD, Cardiology

Clinical Significance

Chest pain accounts for 6-8% of emergency department visits, with cardiac causes representing 15-25% of cases. This guide differentiates life-threatening conditions (ACS, PE) from benign etiologies using evidence-based risk stratification.

Immediate Emergency Criteria

  • Crushing substernal pain >20 minutes
  • Radiation to jaw/left arm
  • Syncope or near-syncope
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Diaphoresis with nausea
  • Known CAD risk factors

Pain Location Characteristics

  • Cardiac: Substernal, radiates to jaw/arm
  • Pulmonary: Pleuritic, worse with inspiration
  • GI: Epigastric, burning quality
  • Musculoskeletal: Reproducible with palpation
  • Neuropathic: Dermatomal distribution

1. Cardiac Causes (15-25% of cases)

Acute Coronary Syndrome

  • STEMI: ST elevation, troponin rise (mortality 5-10%)
  • NSTEMI: Non-ST elevation, biomarker positive
  • Unstable Angina: Normal biomarkers

Key Feature: Levine's sign (clenched fist over sternum)

ECG Changes: ST elevation/depression, T inversion, Q waves

Other Cardiac Causes

Condition Clinical Features Diagnostics
Pericarditis Positional pain, friction rub Diffuse ST elevation, PR depression
Aortic Dissection Tearing pain, pulse deficits CTA chest, widened mediastinum

2. Pulmonary Causes (5-10% of cases)

Pulmonary Embolism

  • Wells Score >4 suggests high probability
  • Pleuritic chest pain + hemoptysis
  • Risk factors: Immobility, cancer, OCP

Pneumothorax

  • Sudden onset dyspnea
  • Hyperresonance to percussion
  • Tall thin males (primary)

3. Gastrointestinal Causes (20-30% of cases)

GERD

  • Retrosternal burning
  • Worse when supine
  • Relieved by antacids

Biliary Colic

  • RUQ pain radiating to back
  • Post-prandial onset
  • Murphy's sign positive

Esophageal Spasm

  • Intermittent squeezing pain
  • May mimic angina
  • Relieved by nitrates

4. Musculoskeletal/Inflammatory Causes (20-35% of cases)

Costochondritis

  • Tenderness at costosternal joints
  • Reproducible with palpation
  • No swelling (vs Tietze syndrome)

Herpes Zoster

  • Dermatomal distribution
  • Vesicular rash appears later
  • Prodrome of burning pain

5. Psychological Causes (10-15% of cases)

Panic Attack

  • Associated with hyperventilation
  • Sense of impending doom
  • No true cardiac features
Diagnosis: Diagnosis of exclusion after ruling out organic causes

Diagnostic Algorithm

  1. Initial Assessment: ABCDE approach, vital signs
  2. ECG: Within 10 minutes of presentation
  3. Cardiac Biomarkers: Troponin at 0h, 3h, 6h
  4. Risk Stratification: HEART, TIMI, GRACE scores
  5. Imaging: CXR, CTA for PE/dissection
